Chart Guide

What each graph is for, when to use it, and why it helps.

This page is a practical guide to the chart types inside the tool. Use it when you know your data but want to choose the right visual for the question you are asking.

How to choose

Start from the question, not the chart

  • If you want to compare groups, start with bar, grouped bar, or stacked bar.
  • If you want to see change over time, start with line or area.
  • If you want to study distributions, start with histogram, density, box, or beeswarm.
  • If you want to study relationships between measures, start with scatter, bubble, or hexbin.
  • If you want to understand structure between columns, use the feature graph.
How to avoid bad picks

What usually goes wrong

  • Pie charts become weak when there are too many categories.
  • Stacked bars are harder than grouped bars when exact segment comparison matters.
  • Scatter plots become noisy when many points overlap; hexbin can help.
  • Density plots show smooth shape, not exact counts.
  • Feature graphs explain field relationships, not row-level stories.
Comparison

Bar chart

Best when you want to compare one metric across categories, places, or short time buckets.

Used for: Comparing totals, counts, rates, or averages across groups.

How to read it: Compare bar heights from one group to another.

Why use it: It is usually the clearest first chart for categorical comparison.

Common use cases: Sales by region, crimes by state, revenue by product, cases by year bucket.

Avoid when: You have too many labels or a continuous numeric X axis.

Comparison

Grouped bar chart

Best when one category is not enough and you need a second split such as region by quarter or department by gender.

Used for: Side-by-side subgroup comparison within each main category.

How to read it: Compare within each cluster first, then compare the same color across clusters.

Why use it: It preserves direct comparison better than stacked bars.

Common use cases: Revenue by region and quarter, survey counts by group and year.

Avoid when: You have too many subgroup levels, because the bars become crowded fast.

Composition

Stacked bar chart

Best when you care about both the total and how that total is made up internally.

Used for: Showing contribution of subgroups inside each category total.

How to read it: First compare full bar height, then compare segment sizes inside each bar.

Why use it: It keeps total and composition in one compact view.

Common use cases: Budget by department and cost type, cases by state and case status.

Avoid when: You need precise subgroup comparison across many categories.

Part-to-whole

Pie chart

Best when you have a few categories and want to show share of a total.

Used for: Share or composition with a small category count.

How to read it: Compare slice sizes, not exact angles.

Why use it: It gives a fast part-to-whole message when the category list is short.

Common use cases: Market share, share of records by one short label set.

Avoid when: Categories are many, similar in size, or not parts of one meaningful whole.

Time trend

Line chart

Best when you want to show how a metric moves across time or any ordered sequence.

Used for: Trend, growth, decline, seasonality, and turning points.

How to read it: Follow the line left to right and watch slope changes.

Why use it: It is the cleanest chart for showing direction over time.

Common use cases: Cases by year, revenue by month, rates over time.

Avoid when: The X axis is unordered categories rather than a true sequence.

Time trend

Area chart

Best when you want a line-like trend but with more visual emphasis on volume or accumulation.

Used for: Trend with stronger emphasis on magnitude.

How to read it: Follow the top edge like a line, but also notice the filled area.

Why use it: It makes the size of the measure feel more present than a plain line.

Common use cases: Revenue over time, prevalence over time, total processed records by month.

Avoid when: You need very precise comparisons or the baseline meaning is weak.

Distribution

Histogram

Best when you want to see how a numeric field is distributed across ranges.

Used for: Shape, spread, skew, clustering, and tails.

How to read it: Look for where bars concentrate and where they thin out.

Why use it: It converts a long numeric list into a readable distribution picture.

Common use cases: Income distribution, case duration ranges, rate spread.

Avoid when: The field is categorical or has too few distinct values.

Distribution

Density plot

Best when you want a smoother view of distribution shape, optionally compared across groups.

Used for: Smooth distribution comparison without visible bins.

How to read it: Higher peaks mean more values gather in that range.

Why use it: It is great for comparing overall shape across groups.

Common use cases: Revenue spread by quarter, exam scores by class, rate distribution by region.

Avoid when: You need exact bin counts or have very little data.

Distribution

Box plot

Best when you want a compact summary of spread, median, and possible outliers.

Used for: Median, interquartile range, and outlier spotting.

How to read it: Focus on the middle box, whiskers, and isolated points.

Why use it: It summarizes a lot of distribution information in very little space.

Common use cases: Income variation, rate stability, processing time spread.

Avoid when: You need to show every row or explain the exact shape of the distribution.

Distribution

Beeswarm plot

Best when you want to see the real spread of individual rows inside categories.

Used for: Row-level spread by category.

How to read it: Compare how high, low, dense, or scattered the dots are inside each category.

Why use it: It keeps the individual records visible while reducing overlap.

Common use cases: Revenue by quarter, scores by section, case duration by district.

Avoid when: The dataset is extremely large and the point cloud becomes too dense.

Relationship

Scatter plot

Best when you want to study the relationship between two numeric measures.

Used for: Correlation, clusters, and outliers.

How to read it: Watch whether points rise together, fall apart, or split into groups.

Why use it: It is the standard chart for two-measure relationships.

Common use cases: Revenue vs orders, population vs incidence, cost vs output.

Avoid when: Point overlap is severe and the plot turns into a dark cloud.

Relationship

Bubble chart

Best when you want to compare three numeric measures at once.

Used for: Two numeric axes plus a third numeric size field.

How to read it: Read X, Y, and bubble size together; color can add a group split.

Why use it: It packs a third measure into the same relationship view.

Common use cases: Orders vs revenue sized by conversion rate, GDP vs population sized by emissions.

Avoid when: Bubble size differences are too subtle or misleadingly dominate the story.

Relationship

Hexbin chart

Best when a scatter plot would be too crowded and you still want to understand concentration patterns.

Used for: High-density two-measure relationships.

How to read it: Darker bins mean more rows overlap in that region.

Why use it: It solves the overplotting problem better than a raw scatter in dense data.

Common use cases: Large transaction data, sensor readings, record-heavy numeric comparisons.

Avoid when: The dataset is tiny and individual dots would tell the story better.

Matrix

Heatmap

Best when you want to compare many row-column combinations at once.

Used for: Two-dimensional comparisons using color intensity.

How to read it: Scan rows and columns, then compare darker and lighter cells.

Why use it: It compresses a large comparison grid into a readable pattern view.

Common use cases: Region by year, category by state, metric by department.

Avoid when: The audience needs exact values more than broad pattern recognition.

Structure

Feature relationship graph

Best when you want to understand how columns in the dataset relate to one another rather than how rows compare.

Used for: Structural understanding of the dataset itself.

How to read it: Each node is a feature, larger nodes are more important, and closer nodes have stronger dependency or association.

Why use it: It helps you discover which columns move together, split the data similarly, or act as central drivers.

Common use cases: Deciding what to chart next, understanding dataset structure before analysis, detecting tightly connected field families.

Avoid when: You want a row-level story or a direct business answer; this chart is about data structure, not a final KPI narrative.
